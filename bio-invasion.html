Biological Invasion

What is Biological Invasion?

Biological invasion occurs when species are introduced intentionally or accidentally into ecosystems outside their natural range. These invasive alien species (IAS) often establish quickly, spread widely, and disrupt native biodiversity. In India and across the globe, invasive species are recognized as one of the leading causes of biodiversity loss, alongside climate change and habitat destruction.

Pathways of Invasive Species Introduction

Invasive plants, animals, and microorganisms spread mainly through human activities, such as:

  • Global trade and transport (shipping, cargo, vehicles, ballast water)
  • Agriculture and aquaculture (exotic crops, fish, and livestock)
  • Horticulture and ornamental planting (garden escapes, landscaping plants)
  • Unintentional releases (pet trade, aquarium plants and fish, escapes from farms)

Once introduced, invasive species often thrive due to the absence of natural predators and their ability to adapt to new habitats.

Why Are Invasive Species a Problem?

The impacts of invasive alien species in India and worldwide are wide-ranging:

  • Environmental impacts: Outcompeting native plants and animals, reducing biodiversity, altering soil, water, and fire regimes.
  • Economic impacts: Lowering agricultural productivity, reducing fish catches, damaging infrastructure, and increasing management costs.
  • Social and cultural impacts: Affecting traditional practices, ecosystem services, and community livelihoods.
  • Human health impacts: Spreading diseases, causing allergies, and introducing harmful pests.

Examples from India include invasive weeds like Lantana camara, Parthenium hysterophorus, and Mikania micrantha , which severely affect forests, farmlands, and pastures.

Managing Biological Invasions

Effective invasive species management involves a combination of strategies:

  • Prevention: Identifying high-risk species and stopping their introduction.
  • Early detection and rapid response (EDRR): Monitoring ecosystems and removing invasive species before they spread.
  • Eradication and control: Using mechanical, chemical, and biological methods to reduce populations.
  • Restoration of native ecosystems: Rehabilitating degraded habitats and reintroducing native species.
  • Community participation: Engaging citizens, researchers, and NGOs in reporting and managing invasions.

Why It Matters

Preventing and controlling invasive alien species in India is essential for protecting biodiversity, ensuring ecological balance, and safeguarding the economy and human wellbeing. Conserving ecosystems from biological invasions also supports climate resilience and sustainable development.

Together, through awareness, research, and collective action, we can reduce the threat of biological invasions and build a healthier planet.

Hypotheses on biological invasion

Biotic Resistance Hypothesis: Ecosystems with high biodiversity resist invasion because competitors, herbivores, and pathogens in the recipient community limit the colonization, naturalization, and persistence of invaders, which are often poorly adapted to indigenous enemies, making competition the primary source of resistance.

Enemy Release Hypothesis: Invasive species succeed in new ranges because they lose the natural enemies that regulated their populations in their native ranges, either through regulatory enemy release, where freedom from enemies directly boosts population growth, or compensatory enemy release, where reduced need for defense allows resources to be redirected toward growth and reproduction.

Tens Rule: Roughly 10% of species transported beyond their native range become introduced, about 10% of introduced species become naturalized, and about 10% of naturalized species become invasive.

Propagule Pressure Hypothesis: High propagule pressure, characterized by larger numbers of individuals per introduction event and more frequent introduction events, increases the likelihood of invasion success by enhancing genetic diversity, continual supplementation, seed swamping, and the probability of arriving in favorable environments.

Ideal Weed Hypothesis: Species with traits such as ruderal life history, small seed size, high plasticity, rapid growth, and high early fecundity and fertility are more likely to invade because these life history strategies give them a competitive advantage over native species.

Increased Susceptibility Hypothesis: Invaders with low genetic diversity and limited defensive adaptations are more susceptible to enemies in the invaded community because genetic bottlenecks and naivety hinder their ability to adapt to new threats.

Specialist–Generalist Hypothesis: Invasion success is promoted when specialized native enemies fail to attack introduced species while generalist mutualists facilitate invaders through broad beneficial interactions.

Novel Weapons Hypothesis: Invaders often gain advantage by releasing novel allelopathic chemicals to which native species are not adapted, suppressing competitors and enhancing invasion success.

Disturbance Hypothesis: Natural or anthropogenic disturbances that increase resource availability and reset succession promote invasion by creating opportunities for ruderal species adept at colonization.

Empty Niche Hypothesis: Invaders succeed in ecosystems with unoccupied niches by exploiting unused resources and introducing novel traits absent in the native community.
